The Ochre City — Brief History

1070
Founded by the Almoravid dynasty (Yusuf ibn Tashfin). The red sandstone walls that give the city its nickname "The Red City" were built in this era. The word "Morocco" in most European languages derives from "Marrakech."
16th c.
Saadian dynasty golden age — the Ben Youssef Madrasa and Saadian Tombs were built. Marrakech became the jewel of North Africa.
1912
French Protectorate begins. Guéliz (La Ville Nouvelle) built alongside the Medina — wide boulevards, contemporary cafes, the world you're staying in at Radisson Blu.
Now
UNESCO World Heritage Medina. Two cities side by side: the 1,000-year labyrinth and the modern French-grid Gueliz. You are based in Gueliz — the Medina is 10–40 min away depending on where you're going.

Food BibleWhat to order, where, and why — TasteAtlas + Arabic local sources

Moroccan food is event-specific and venue-specific. Eating the right thing in the right place beats a 90-item tourist menu every time. These notes cross-check TasteAtlas, Wikivoyage FR, Hespress, and SNRT Arabic.

Marrakech Exclusive

الطنجية المراكشية · Tanjia Marrakchia

Marrakech's bachelor dish: meat, preserved lemon, saffron, cumin, and smen sealed in a clay urn and slow-cooked in the embers of a public hammam furnace (the faran). You cannot get this in Fes or Casablanca. Order it wherever you see the clay pot.

Order: beef or lamb tanjia, khobz, mint tea. Ask the price before they cook it.

WhereMechoui Alley near Jemaa el-Fna, Riad Zitoun area, or ask hotel for nearest specialist
Price range60–120 MAD for a good portion. 300+ MAD tourist price — walk away.

Cooked Salads & Breads

Zaalouk + Taktouka زعلوك وتكتوكة

Cooked salads are not side noise. Zaalouk: smoky eggplant + tomato. Taktouka: roasted pepper + tomato. Both scooped with bread.

Order before grills — 15–25 MAD each
Msemen / Meloui مسمن / ملوي

Layered griddle breads. Msemen is square and flaky; meloui is coiled. Best hot with amlou (argan oil + almond paste + honey).

Msemen, amlou, nous-nous coffee
Bissara بيصارة

Fava bean soup with olive oil, cumin, chilli. Classic morning-to-midday street food. Filling, cheap, local. Find a pot in the medina.

5–10 MAD · street food staple
Atay · Nous-nous أتاي / نوص نوص

Mint tea is hospitality and theatre. Nous-nous is half-espresso, half-milk: the Gueliz local coffee order. Ask for less sugar if needed.

Ask for sugar on the side

Local HacksSmart moves drawn from local Arabic forums, Wikivoyage, and expat advice

Hack 01 · Transport

Use InDrive or Roby — Not Street Taxis

Taxi drivers in Marrakech are notorious for refusing the meter and overcharging tourists. The local fix: download inDrive or Roby (Moroccan Uber equivalents). You get a transparent, pre-agreed price before you get in — no negotiation, no scam.

Still in a street taxi? Say "Compteur s'il vous plaît" before closing the door. If they refuse or claim it's broken, walk away.
Hack 02 · Navigation

The Stationary Direction Rule

Never ask a young man walking on the street for directions in the Medina — this is the fastest route to a "faux guide" who will demand a tip for walking you somewhere.

Only ask someone who is stationary and cannot leave their post: a shopkeeper inside their store, a waiter at a cafe, or a police officer.
Hack 03 · Souks

The 50% Haggling Rule

Haggling is cultural — not haggling is almost insulting. Start your counter-offer at 30–50% of their initial quoted price. Be friendly, smile, and walk away if the price isn't right. 9 times out of 10, they will call you back with a better number.

Anchor yourself first: visit the Ensemble Artisanal (government-regulated, fixed-price artisan complex) to see what fair prices look like before entering the souks.
Hack 04 · Cash

The Two-Pocket Cash Trick

Never pull out a large wad of cash for a small purchase. Vendors often claim they have no change for large bills, forcing you to tip the difference or buy more.

Keep 20–50 MAD notes in an accessible front pocket for daily purchases. Keep your main cash hidden. Bring plenty of small coins — many stalls cannot break 100+ MAD notes.
Hack 05 · Sightseeing

Maison de la Photographie — Best Rooftop in the Medina

Tucked in a beautiful riad, this museum has vintage photographs of Morocco from the 19th–20th century. The exhibition is interesting — but the real reason to go is the rooftop terrace: mint tea + panoramic views of the Atlas Mountains. Usually quiet even when the main sites are packed.

Map → Check opening hours in advance.
Hack 06 · Souks

Souk Semmarine vs. Bab El Khemis

Souk Semmarine is beautiful but highly commercialised — the main tourist artery where prices are inflated and everything is performative. Bab El Khemis flea market (Thursday mornings) is raw, local, zero tourist curation, and where things are much cheaper.

For artisan workshops where things are actually made: wander off the main souk streets into the hidden alleys. Prices drop dramatically the further you get from the tourist path.
Hack 07 · Hammam

Know Your Hammam Tier Before You Go

Local quartier hammam: 15–30 MAD. Spartan, public, authentic. Bring your own savon beldi, kessa glove, flip-flops. About hygiene and socialising.
Tourist spa hammam: 150–600+ MAD. Private, luxurious, English-speaking staff.

Book in advance via official website or ask your hotel. Never follow a stranger's recommendation to a "great nearby hammam" — they are collecting a commission. Confirm the full price before undressing.
Hack 08 · Dress

Cover Up in the Medina

Marrakech is relatively liberal but it is a conservative culture. In the Medina, cover shoulders and knees. You will attract significantly less unwanted attention and show basic respect.

Gueliz (where you're based) is more relaxed. The deeper into the Medina, the more it matters.

Shopping IntelWhat to buy, what to test, what to avoid — argan, saffron, rugs, leather

Amlou — "Moroccan Nutella"

The hidden gem buy that tourists miss because they chase expensive argan oil. Amlou is a rich, nutritious spread of toasted almonds, argan oil, and honey. Absolutely delicious on msemen. Cheap, light to carry, genuinely local.

Buy at a local food shop or market stall. No fancy packaging needed — any pot from a local vendor is authentic.

Argan Oil — The Absorption Test

The vast majority of "pure" argan oil sold by street vendors and in main souks is heavily diluted with cheap vegetable oil. Real argan oil is expensive to produce.

Test: Pure cosmetic argan oil absorbs into the skin almost instantly and leaves no greasy film. Diluted oil sits on top.
Buy only from certified women's cooperatives or established pharmacies. If the price seems like a bargain, it is 100% fake.

Saffron — The Water Test

Vendors regularly sell dyed corn silk, safflower, or colored synthetic threads as saffron. Never buy powdered saffron — it is too easily faked.

Test: Drop threads in warm water. Real saffron slowly releases golden-yellow color. Instant bright red = dye. Also: real threads have a complex earthy/metallic aroma.
Look for the official Moroccan label: IGP "Safran de Taliouine". No label = skip it.

Berber Rugs — The Imperfection Test

True handwoven Berber rugs (Beni Ouarain, Kilim, Boucherouite) have patterns that tell stories from the weaver's life.

Test: Look for slight imperfections in the pattern. Perfect symmetry = factory-produced, not handwoven on a traditional loom. Imperfection = authentic.
High-pressure carpet pitch with tea and "my cousin's shop" = tourist commission trap. Find a standalone workshop instead.

Leather — The Smell Test

Marrakech leather (babouches, poufs, bags) is world-class when done properly.

Test: High-quality traditionally-tanned leather smells earthy and natural. Cheap rushed tanning smells strongly of chemicals or ammonia.
Tannery "free views" with mint gifts always lead to a shop pitch with commission prices built in.

Ensemble Artisanal — Price Anchor

Government-regulated complex where local artisans sell at fixed, fair prices. Not the cheapest place to buy — but the best place to learn what a fair price looks like before entering the souks.

Visit here first. Photograph or note prices. Then haggle in the souks starting at 50% of what you saw here.

Tourist Trap Field Guide8 scenarios with counter-moves

Trap 01

The Unofficial Guide

A friendly local "just happens" to walk your direction and offers to show you "the real souks." After 10 minutes you are in a cousin's shop with social pressure to buy.

Counter: "La, shukran" calmly, no eye contact, keep walking. Repeat once if needed. Do not apologise — it invites re-engagement.
Trap 02

Road Closed / Festival Detour

Someone tells you your destination is closed or there's a festival today. It is always a reroute into a shop.

Counter: Ask a shopkeeper independently. Google Maps still works. The "closure" evaporates when you show a phone map.
Trap 03

The Free Salad

You sit, an un-ordered spread arrives. When the bill comes it includes every item. Sometimes billed per person.

Counter: Ask for a menu with prices before sitting. If a free item arrives, ask explicitly if it's included or refuse it politely.
Trap 04

Tannery "Free View" Mint Gift

Someone offers a sprig of mint and leads you to a tannery viewpoint. After the view a shop pitch begins.

Counter: Take nothing free near tanneries. Enter only through official viewpoint. Afternoons after 17:00 are especially aggressive.
Trap 05

Photo Demand After the Fact

Henna artists, snake handlers, and dressed performers move into your shot. Once photographed they demand 50–200 MAD.

Counter: Agree price before any photo involving a person or animal. Zero exceptions. If they demand after, offer 5–10 MAD and walk.
Trap 06

Only Today Carpet

In a carpet shop: "this price is only today, tomorrow my brother arrives and takes it." Time pressure is manufactured.

Counter: Never buy under time pressure. Walk out, see how urgently they follow. Price drops 30–40% outside the door.
Trap 07

The Taxi Detour

Driver "knows a better restaurant" or "your hotel has moved" — both lead to a commission stop.

Counter: Tell the driver the exact address before the door closes. "Radisson Blu Carré Eden, direct." You can refuse to pay for an unauthorised detour.
Trap 08

Argan Cooperative Cream

A "women's argan cooperative" with a free demonstration. Everything is real until the pitch for 800+ MAD cosmetics begins.

Counter: Buy argan oil only at a pharmacy or labelled food shop. 30 MAD culinary argan vs 800 MAD "beauty" are not different products.
Trap 09

The Unsolicited Henna Tattoo

Women in the square suddenly grab your hand and start drawing a henna tattoo without permission, then demand payment. Worse: "black henna" (PPD dye) is toxic and causes severe allergic reactions — including chemical burns and permanent scarring.

Counter: Keep hands in pockets or close to your body walking through that section. If grabbed, pull away firmly immediately. Never accept black henna — real henna is always orange-brown, never black.
Trap 10

The Fake Saffron / Fake Spice Pitch

Vendors regularly sell dyed corn silk or colored synthetic threads as "pure saffron." Powdered saffron is almost always fake. Other spice blends are often cheap fillers with a few real threads on top for visual effect.

Counter: See Shopping Intel section. Only buy threads (not powder), test in warm water, look for IGP Taliouine label. Buy from a pharmacy, not a market stall pitch.
Trap 11

The "Sahara in a Day" Desert Tour

Street agencies near Jemaa el-Fna sell cheap "Sahara Desert" tours for 1–2 days. What they call Sahara is often the Agafay Desert — a flat, rocky wasteland outside Marrakech. The real sand dunes (Erg Chebbi, Merzouga) are a 9–10 hour drive each way.

Counter: If you want real dunes, book a minimum 3-day/2-night tour with an established, reviewed operator before arriving. Any "Sahara" trip sold on the street for under 800 MAD is either fake dunes or a scam.
