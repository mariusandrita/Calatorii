# Traditional Food of Marrakech: A Culinary Guide

Based on insights from TasteAtlas, Wikivoyage (in multiple languages), and local Arabic/French food forums (e.g., Bladi, Reddit, local Google Maps reviews).

*Filtered out tourist traps. Recommendations based on local consensus.*

## Must-Try Traditional Foods & Where to Find Them

### 1. Tanjia (الطنجية المراكشية)
The quintessential Marrakech dish. Slow-cooked meat (lamb or beef) with preserved lemon, garlic, cumin, saffron, and smen (fermented butter) in a clay urn nestled in hammam embers.

**Where locals eat it:**
- **Chez Lamine Hadj Mustapha (حاج مصطفى):** Derb Semmarine near Jemaa el-Fna. Historic institution for authentic Tanjia and roasted meats.
- **Cafe Tiznit (مقهى طنجيت):** Behind the Olive Market. Very local, non-touristy. Famous for tanjia cooked the traditional way.
- **The "Farran" Experience:** DIY tanjia — buy meat from a local butcher, mix spices in an urn, pay the Farran a few dirhams to slow-cook it in the hammam ashes overnight.

### 2. Tagine (الطاجين)
**Where locals eat it:**
- **Le Chaabi (مطعم الشعبي):** Budget-friendly canteen praised on Arabic forums. Best for kefta and egg tagine.
- **Dar L'hssira (دار الحصيرة):** Hidden from tourist noise in the Medina. Authentic, well-priced tagines.
- **Chez Bejgueni (بجويني):** Gueliz local favorite. Charcoal grills, traditional veal and vegetable tagines.

### 3. Couscous (الكسكسي)
**Local Rule:** Only order couscous on a Friday afternoon — this is when locals eat it fresh after midday prayers. Reheated tourist couscous any other day is not the same dish.

**Where locals eat it:**
- **Naima Couscous (نعيمة الكسكسي):** Small cantine in the Medina, feels like a Moroccan family's home. Set menu, sharing platters.
- **La Cantine des Gazelles:** Excellent quality-to-price couscous, recommended in French local reviews.

### 4. Pastilla / B'stilla (الباستيلا)
Sweet-and-savory layered pastry: spiced pigeon or chicken, almonds, eggs, warka dough, cinnamon, and powdered sugar.

**Where locals eat it:**
- **Patisserie Al Jaouda (الجودة):** Local Medina bakery. Chicken pastillas to go — bakery prices.
- **Café Guerrab:** High-quality pastilla at local prices, not tourist rates.
- **Al Fassia (الفاسية):** Women-run institution in Gueliz. The gold standard for pigeon pastilla.

### 5. Harira (الحريرة) & Street Food
Tomato-based soup with lentils, chickpeas, vermicelli. Other: snail soup (Bobouch), Msemen (flaky flatbreads), Maakouda (fried potato cakes).

**Where locals eat it:**
- **Jemaa el-Fnaa Stalls:** Golden rule — find stalls crowded with Moroccan families. Stall #14 = fried seafood. Others = harira + dates.
- **Bab El Khemis Market:** Raw, authentic. Kerouin (trotters with chickpeas), harira. No tourist crowds.
